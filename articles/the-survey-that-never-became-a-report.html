The Survey That Never Became a Report

6 min read · Field teams & NGOs · May 2025
EN ES

There is a particular kind of organizational failure that nobody talks about, because it is invisible. A team plans a field campaign. The forms get designed, the volunteers get trained, the logistics get sorted. People travel to remote communities and spend weeks gathering responses from hundreds of households. And then the data — all of it, carefully collected — quietly disappears.

Not deleted. Not lost in a flood. Just never processed. Never analyzed. Never turned into the report it was supposed to become.

It happens more than most development organizations will admit. The causes are rarely dramatic. No system crashed. No one made a catastrophic mistake. The failure is quieter than that — a combination of staff turnover, tools that don't match the actual conditions of fieldwork, and a persistent gap between the moment data is collected and the moment it might be used.

The handoff problem

One of the most consistent causes is something mundane: people leave.

Field programs depend on institutional knowledge in ways that are easy to underestimate. The coordinator who ran last year's survey knew how the GPS coordinates were recorded and why some household IDs skipped numbers. She knew which village names had been abbreviated and what those abbreviations meant. When she moved on in December, that knowledge went with her. The forms stayed behind, in a folder that someone else would open months later and struggle to interpret.

This is the handoff problem, and it is chronic in the development sector, where project cycles are short, funding is uncertain, and staff turnover is high. Paper forms make it worse. A paper form has no metadata, no search function, no built-in explanation of how it was designed or what the responses mean. It is, in a sense, inseparable from the person who created it. When that person leaves, the form becomes an artifact — evidence that work happened, but not something you can easily build on.

The connectivity assumption

Digital tools were supposed to fix this. And for many organizations they have — the ones that work in offices, with reliable internet, and with field teams that collect data in places where a mobile signal can be assumed.

For everyone else, digital tools introduced a different problem: they were built for a world that doesn't exist in the field. Submit a Google Form with no connection and the response disappears. Many of the purpose-built survey platforms have offline modes, but they are cloud-first products designed for enterprise budgets, with pricing that puts them out of reach for a small health NGO or a community research team.

The result is that the choice for most small field teams has been: paper, or a tool that half-works offline and stores all your data on someone else's servers. Neither option is actually good. Paper is universally available but brittle — dependent on legibility, on not getting rained on, on the person filling it out understanding the question the same way the person who designed it did. Cloud tools are powerful but fragile in a different way: they assume a kind of connectivity that rural fieldwork cannot guarantee.

A different starting assumption

What some teams are discovering is that the answer isn't a better paper form or a more robust cloud product. It's a different starting assumption: what if offline is the default, not the exception?

A small number of tools are built around this premise — designed for situations where connectivity is unreliable, where data needs to be collected in the field and analyzed later, and where the organization, not a third-party platform, owns what gets gathered. Questbee is one of them. It's free, it runs on hardware you already have, and it works just as well with no internet as with a fast connection. Field workers collect data on Android phones that store everything locally. When they reconnect, data syncs automatically — and it arrives already structured: GPS coordinates export to any mapping tool, photos are indexed to their source records, routes export as GPX tracks. Nothing goes to an external server.

What matters more than the specific tool, though, is the shift in thinking it represents. An organization that treats offline as the baseline ends up with data that is searchable, transferable, and independent of the people who collected it. When a coordinator leaves in December, the next person opens the same dashboard and sees every campaign, every response, every GPS point the team has ever recorded. The data no longer belongs to whoever happened to collect it. It belongs to the organization.

What actually changes

The cardboard box sitting in the storage room is a failure of infrastructure, not of effort. The people who filled those forms worked hard. The coordinator who designed the campaign was meticulous. The data they collected might have been genuinely useful — might still be, if someone could make sense of it. But it was collected in a way that made it fragile: tied to one person's knowledge, stored in a format that doesn't travel well, in an organization where that person eventually left.

When teams start treating field data the way they treat other organizational assets — stored centrally, accessible to anyone on the team, independent of individual knowledge — things change. Not just for the current campaign, but for every one that follows. Past surveys become searchable. Patterns become visible across years, not just months. New staff inherit context instead of cardboard boxes. And the data that comes back from the field is already structured for use — not a pile of files that needs cleanup before it can mean anything.

The data collected in the field is often the most direct evidence an organization has of what is actually happening in the communities it works with. It tends to be hard-won — expensive in time, in travel, in the trust of the people who answered the questions. It deserves infrastructure that treats it accordingly.

If you want to see what that looks like in practice, Questbee is worth a look — and the setup takes about 30 minutes.
