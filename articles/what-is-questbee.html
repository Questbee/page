The Best Field Survey Tool You've Never Heard Of

7 min read · Field teams & NGOs · May 2025
EN ES

Six months ago, a small health organization ran their most ambitious campaign yet — 140 households across three rural communities, a dataset that was supposed to shape their programs for the next two years. The field coordinator who designed the forms, trained the volunteers, and managed the whole effort was experienced and meticulous. In November she received a better offer. By December she was gone.

The paper forms were handed to Sarah — a quick briefing, a promise to be reachable by email, and a folder labeled with a campaign name she hadn't worked on. The abbreviations made sense if you'd been in the field. The coding scheme was intuitive if you'd invented it. The GPS coordinates needed to be cross-referenced with a notebook that was somewhere in the office, probably in one of the boxes in the back room.

Four months later, the analysis still hadn't started. It was quietly shelved. Three months of fieldwork and 140 household surveys produced no report, no findings, and no decisions. The data still exists — in a cardboard box in a storage room — but it belongs to no one anymore.

"This happens more than people admit," says Sarah. She is now coordinating the next campaign for the same communities. This time, she's making sure the data outlasts the people who collect it.

There is a way to do that. It's called Questbee, it's completely free, and most field teams have never heard of it.

The problem with how most teams collect data

Field data collection has always forced a choice between two imperfect options. Paper is cheap and works anywhere, but it's fragile, slow to process, and full of transcription errors by the time someone types it all in. Digital tools solve those problems but introduce a new one: they assume you always have internet. In a city office, that's a reasonable assumption. In the field — 80 km from the nearest cell tower, or in a rural health clinic with a connection that drops twice a day — it isn't.

Most teams patch something together. They use Google Forms when there's a signal and paper when there isn't, then spend hours reconciling the two. Some use WhatsApp to photograph forms and send them back to the office. Some build elaborate spreadsheets that technically work until three people are editing them at once. These workarounds are creative, but they're also fragile — and every gap in the process is a place where data can disappear.

The tools built to solve this properly — platforms designed for offline field data collection — exist, but they tend to fall into one of two categories: expensive enterprise software that requires months of setup and an IT team to maintain, or complicated open-source projects with steep learning curves and little support. Neither option fits a small team with a real problem and a Tuesday deadline.

What Questbee actually is

Questbee is a complete field data collection platform: a web dashboard where you build and manage your forms, and an Android app your team uses in the field. The app works fully offline — no internet required, no connection dropped mid-survey, no data lost. When your team gets back and connects to the network, everything syncs automatically and shows up in your dashboard.

What makes it different from the tools most teams have tried is where the data lives. With Questbee, it stays on your own machine — a laptop, a small server, whatever you already have. Nothing goes to Google. Nothing goes to a cloud service you don't control. Your data is yours, stored where you put it, accessible to anyone on your team — not just the person who collected it.

It's also free. Not a free trial, not a limited tier, not "free for up to 5 users." Free. The entire platform, with no caps on forms, responses, or devices. It's built and maintained as an open project, and anyone can use it.

How a team actually uses it

The setup takes about 30 minutes the first time. After that, the day-to-day is simple enough that a non-technical program coordinator can run it without help.

You design your forms in a web browser on your computer — the same way you'd build a Google Form, but without needing anyone to be online. You can add text fields, number inputs, dropdown choices, date fields, GPS coordinates, route tracking, photo and audio capture, barcode and QR scanning, digital signatures, and repeat groups for capturing multiple observations within a single interview. When the form is ready, you publish it.

Your field workers carry Android phones — any Android phone, nothing special required. Before going out, they open the Questbee app, which automatically downloads the latest published forms. In the field, they fill out each survey the same way they would on any app. The phone stores everything locally as they go. There's no loading, no spinning wheel, no "connection failed" error.

When they return and the phone connects to your network, the data syncs in the background. By the time they've put the phone down and had a coffee, you're looking at their submissions in your dashboard.

Who it's for

Questbee was built for teams that do real fieldwork — people who visit households, inspect sites, monitor conditions, or collect responses in places where connectivity is unreliable or nonexistent. That covers a wide range of work: public health surveys, agricultural monitoring, environmental sampling, community assessments, school inspections, humanitarian response data collection.

The common thread is a team that goes out into the world to gather information, and a coordinator who needs that information to be accurate, complete, and usable. If that sounds like your work, Questbee was built for you.

It's also worth saying what Questbee is not. It's not a tool for sending surveys to people over the internet. It's not a replacement for Google Forms in a conference room with Wi-Fi. It's specifically for the situation where your data collectors are physically in the field, and the field doesn't have a reliable connection.

Thirty minutes of setup. A problem that never comes back.

Sarah set up Questbee on a Tuesday afternoon. Thirty minutes from start to finish. The next time her team went to the field, everything was different — not because the work was different, but because the data no longer depended on any one person to survive.

Every form was filled out on a phone. Every GPS coordinate was captured automatically. When the field workers connected to the office network that evening, all 140 submissions appeared in the dashboard — organized, searchable, and there. No transcription. No notebooks to cross-reference. No coding scheme that only made sense to the person who invented it. The GPS data was ready to map. The photos were indexed to their source records.

And if Sarah leaves tomorrow, her replacement opens the same dashboard and sees everything: every campaign, every response, every data point the team has ever collected. The data doesn't belong to the person who gathered it. It belongs to the organization.

That's what Questbee actually changes. Not just how data is collected, but how long it lasts — and who it belongs to when the person who collected it moves on.
